"Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation on Cognitive Function in Alzheimer's Disease: A Randomized Controlled Trial."
Abstract: Objective: To investigate the effect of vitamin D supplementation on cognitive function in Alzheimer's disease (AD) patients. Methods: This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial included 80 AD patients who were randomly assigned to receive either vitamin D (n = 40) or placebo (n = 40) for 24 weeks. Cognitive function was assessed at baseline and at the end of the study using the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) and the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA). Results: The mean baseline MMSE and MoCA scores were similar between the two groups. However, at the end of the study, the mean MMSE and MoCA scores were significantly higher in the vitamin D group than in the placebo group (p < 0.001). Conclusion: Vitamin D supplementation may have a beneficial effect on cognitive function in AD patients.

"The Relationship between Sleep Disorders and Alzheimer's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis."
Abstract: Background: Sleep disorders are common in patients with Alzheimer's disease (AD). However, the relationship between sleep disorders and AD remains unclear. This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to investigate the relationship between sleep disorders and AD. Methods: We searched PubMed, Embase, and the Cochrane Library for relevant studies published up to August 2022. The quality of the included studies was assessed using the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS). Results: A total of 23 studies were included in this meta-analysis. The pooled results showed that patients with AD had a higher prevalence of sleep disorders than controls (OR = 3.13, 95% CI 2.35-4.17, p < 0.001). Subgroup analysis showed that the prevalence of sleep disorders was higher in patients with moderate to severe AD than in those with mild AD (OR = 3.52, 95% CI 1.90-6.54, p < 0.001). Conclusion: Sleep disorders are common in patients with AD, and the prevalence of sleep disorders is higher in patients with moderate to severe AD than in those with mild AD.

"Association of Cognitive Impairment with Brain White Matter Hyperintensities in Patients with Alzheimer's Disease."
Abstract: Background: White matter hyperintensities (WMH) are frequently observed on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in patients with Alzheimer's disease (AD). However, the relationship between WMH and cognitive impairment in AD remains unclear. Methods: This study included 78 patients with AD who underwent MRI scans and cognitive assessments. WMH volume was quantified using an automated software. Cognitive impairment was assessed using the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) and the Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scale. Results: The results showed that WMH volume was significantly correlated with MMSE scores (r = -0.55, p < 0.001) and CDR scores (r = 0.47, p < 0.001) in patients with AD. Multiple linear regression analysis showed that WMH volume was an independent predictor of cognitive impairment in AD after adjusting for age, sex, education, and other confounding factors (β = -0.43, p < 0.001). Conclusion: WMH may play an important role in cognitive impairment in patients with AD, and WMH volume could be a potential biomarker for predicting cognitive decline in AD.

Title: Prevalence and Clinical Correlates of Olfactory Dysfunction in Patients with Alzheimer's Disease and Comorbid Depression
Abstract: Olfactory dysfunction is a common finding in patients with Alzheimer's disease (AD) and has been associated with depression in non-demented populations. We aimed to determine the prevalence and clinical correlates of olfactory dysfunction in AD patients with comorbid depression. A total of 65 AD patients with comorbid depression were compared to 55 age- and gender-matched AD patients without depression. Olfactory function was assessed with the University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test (UPSIT). The prevalence of olfactory dysfunction was significantly higher in AD patients with depression compared to those without (69.2% vs 29.1%, p<0.001). In multivariable analyses, lower UPSIT scores were associated with worse cognitive and functional status, greater depressive symptom severity, and younger age. Our findings suggest that olfactory dysfunction is a common and clinically relevant feature of AD patients with comorbid depression.

Title: Comorbid Depression and the Risk of Dementia in Alzheimer's Disease Patients: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
Abstract: Depression is a common comorbidity in Alzheimer's disease (AD) patients, but its relationship with the risk of dementia in this population is unclear. We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of longitudinal studies that investigated the association between comorbid depression and the risk of dementia in AD patients. A total of 14 studies were included in the meta-analysis, representing 17,066 AD patients with a mean follow-up of 3.9 years. The pooled results showed that comorbid depression was associated with a significantly increased risk of dementia in AD patients (hazard ratio [HR] = 1.62, 95% confidence interval [CI] = 1.38-1.90, p < 0.001). Subgroup analyses indicated that the association was stronger in studies with longer follow-up and in those that used clinical diagnoses of depression. Our findings suggest that comorbid depression is a significant risk factor for dementia in AD patients and highlight the importance of early detection and treatment of depression in this population.

Title: "The association between osteoporosis and Alzheimer's disease: a systematic review and meta-analysis"
Abstract: BACKGROUND: Both osteoporosis and Alzheimer's disease (AD) are common chronic diseases that are frequently seen in elderly individuals. However, the association between the two diseases remains unclear. METHODS: A systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies were performed to assess the association between osteoporosis and AD. RESULTS: A total of 17 studies were included in the meta-analysis, with a total of 270,856 participants. The pooled results showed that osteoporosis was associated with an increased risk of AD (pooled OR 1.52, 95% CI 1.27-1.82, p < 0.001). CONCLUSIONS: The findings of this study suggest that there is a positive association between osteoporosis and AD.
